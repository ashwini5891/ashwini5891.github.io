Building a Platform for Charity Groups

Helping charity teams simplify finances and focus on impact

Problem space

Across the UK, there are more than 12,000 charity groups with teams of 2 to 10 people. These groups are the backbone of local life, improving playgrounds, running fundraising fairs, or protecting community green spaces such as Friends of Highgate Woods in London.

Operations are fragmented:

  • Finances live in spreadsheets
  • Project management happens in shared documents
  • Communication runs through WhatsApp threads

Most members are volunteers balancing day jobs and family commitments. Many groups handle £5,000 to £15,000 in annual turnover, with a significant portion lost to platform fees during fundraising.

General tools like QuickBooks or Asana feel too broad. Niche tools such as Classlist do not cover the full workflow end to end. These charities are the unsung heroes of the community, and they deserve better tools, not corporate complexity.

Team size

2 to 10 members

Turnover

£5,000 to £15,000 per year

Pain

High admin effort and platform fees

Examples include PTAs, Friends of parks charities, and local environmental groups.

Product opportunity

The vision was a unified platform that simplifies financial management, event coordination, and administrative tasks for charity groups.

The goal: Reduce financial upkeep, automate manual reporting, and help charity teams spend more time fundraising and less time balancing spreadsheets.

If we save five hours per month for each group, that equates to more than 700,000 volunteer hours freed annually across the UK.

How it started

  • Create and manage fundraising events
  • Record income and expenses
  • Track spending against event budgets

We shipped a clean financial dashboard that summarised cash flow by month and event.

After launch, there was silence. No sign ups. No activity. The product existed, but distribution and validated demand did not.

Challenges

1. Distribution

We fell into the classic build it and they will come trap. Charity groups were hard to reach at the point of need.

2. Access to users

Cold outreach underperformed. In Facebook and WhatsApp groups, organisers were polite but reluctant to trial new software.

3. Cost to serve

Infrastructure costs were disproportionate to small charity budgets. Even modest subscriptions felt heavy.

4. Data migration

Years of spreadsheet history made switching feel costly. The perceived effort outweighed the value.

The pivot

We started again, smaller and closer to users. We removed cost and noise by shutting down the full SaaS, the landing pages, and paid ads. We embedded in niche charity networks, focused on trust, and listened. In parallel, we mapped the end to end journey to find a beachhead problem.

Insights

Finances
Tracking funds and platform fees
Weight: 70 percent
Event management
Calendars and volunteers
Weight: 20 percent
Gift Aid
Confusing paperwork
Weight: 10 percent

Weights reflect relative frequency and intensity of complaints during discovery.

MVPs

1. Finance tracker

A lightweight web prototype for CSV uploads from banks or payment processors. It reconciled transactions and categorised income and expenses by event and month for instant visibility.

Result: In the first month, 27 charity groups used it regularly and reported fewer spreadsheet hours per event cycle.

2. Event calendar

A Notion template that combined a calendar, reminders, and task tracking. Shared in charity spaces and passed along by organisers.

Adoption: about 140 downloads in the first few weeks through word of mouth.

3. Gift Aid handling

Deprioritised due to adequate free tools in the market. Focus remained on financial workflows where pain and opportunity were highest.

Building a community

We went door to door to meet charity teams, asked what they needed, and observed how they ran activities. We built relationships first, features second.

In parallel, we shared useful content such as fundraising tips, reporting templates, and policy updates. No product push, only practical help.

  • 400 percent increase in engagement across posts
  • Three times more inbound conversations about finance problems
  • Dozens of new users for the finance MVP through referrals

Where things stand

  • Zero ad spend with steady organic adoption
  • Retention above 70 percent among MVP testers
  • Active feedback loop guiding automation and reporting

The work suggests demand for a sector specific finance tool written in the language of charities, not accountants.

What is next

  • Automated reconciliation via open banking connections
  • Integrated fundraising with lower platform fees
  • Simple dashboards for spending, donations, and upcoming events

Long term, the aim is an operating system for charities that saves time, reduces cost, and lets them focus on impact.
